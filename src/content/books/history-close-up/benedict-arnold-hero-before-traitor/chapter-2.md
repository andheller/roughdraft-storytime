# Chapter 2: Ticonderoga, Quebec, and Valcour Island

In the first years of the Revolution, Benedict Arnold seemed to appear wherever the fighting was most difficult.

At Fort Ticonderoga in May 1775, he joined Ethan Allen and the Green Mountain Boys in seizing a British stronghold that guarded the route between Canada and the Hudson Valley. The victory brought badly needed cannon and supplies into American hands. It was an early success full of drama, the kind of event a revolution needs in order to believe in itself. Arnold helped make it happen. He also immediately fell into disputes over rank, authority, and credit. The pattern was becoming familiar. He was useful, brave, and hard to manage.

Later that same year, George Washington approved one of the boldest plans of the war: an American expedition through the Maine wilderness to attack Quebec. Arnold commanded the force. The march itself became legendary for hardship. Men hacked through forest, dragged heavy bateaux across difficult routes, and endured hunger, cold, illness, and collapsing supplies. Many turned back or became too weak to continue. The expedition was a test of endurance almost beyond reason.

Arnold drove it forward.

This is one of the places where his strengths shine most clearly. He could demand terrible effort and continue under conditions that would have broken a softer commander. He believed in action even when action seemed almost impossible. Under him, the shattered survivors reached Quebec and linked with the forces of Richard Montgomery.

The attack on the city on December 31, 1775, failed. Montgomery was killed. Arnold was badly wounded in the leg. The assault did not take Quebec for the Americans, but Arnold's conduct won admiration. Again he had shown courage under extreme conditions, and again he carried away both reputation and grievance. He had nearly achieved something immense and had instead received pain, failure, and frustration.

Many military careers would have faded after such a campaign. Arnold's did not.

In 1776 he turned to a different challenge: controlling Lake Champlain. This may not sound as famous as Saratoga, but it was strategically vital. The lake-and-river route from Canada into New York offered Britain a natural invasion corridor. If the British moved down that line too quickly, they could split the rebellious colonies and place huge pressure on the American cause.

Arnold understood that the Americans could not simply wait on shore for the enemy to appear. He helped build a small improvised fleet on the lake. This required not only military courage, but organization, labor, and technical improvisation. Ships had to be constructed and armed under severe limitations. The result was not a grand navy. It was a stopgap force built to delay a stronger enemy.

At the Battle of Valcour Island in October 1776, Arnold's little fleet fought the British in a losing tactical engagement. Many of his vessels were destroyed or captured. Yet here again the simple word "lost" can mislead. The battle delayed the British advance and helped prevent a full-scale invasion from moving south that year. In strategic terms, Arnold had accomplished something important even while giving way on the water.

This combination of daring and larger effect helps explain why Washington valued him. Arnold could act in extreme situations and often understood that an apparent local defeat might still serve a wider purpose if it bought time or position. He was not merely rash. He could see the campaign beyond the skirmish.

Yet his relationships with other officers kept worsening. Arnold's courage did not make him diplomatic. He accused rivals of jealousy and incompetence, and sometimes he was right. At other times his own pride made compromise impossible. A revolutionary army is always partly political. Men argue over rank, seniority, rewards, and reputation because those things shape authority and future power. Arnold never handled such matters calmly.

This tension reached a painful point in early 1777, when Congress promoted several officers over him. Arnold felt humiliated. To a man already alert to insult, this was not a small slight. Washington persuaded him to remain in service, recognizing both his value and his volatility.

Arnold did remain, and in 1777 he helped repel a British raid at Danbury, Connecticut, again fighting with exceptional courage. His military importance remained undeniable. He was promoted major general, but Congress did not restore his seniority immediately, which meant the wound to his honor still felt open.

By the time the northern campaign of 1777 reached Saratoga, Arnold was already a man carrying resentment alongside achievement. That bitterness had not yet broken his loyalty, but it was accumulating.

Then came the battle that should have fixed his name in American glory forever.

Instead it became the last great moment before his life turned dark.
