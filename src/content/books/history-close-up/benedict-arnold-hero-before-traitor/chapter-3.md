# Chapter 3: Saratoga and the Wound That Became a Monument

If Benedict Arnold had died at Saratoga in October 1777, the United States might remember him almost entirely as a hero.

That is not exaggeration.

The Saratoga campaign was one of the great turning points of the American Revolution. British General John Burgoyne advanced south from Canada, hoping to cut New England off from the rest of the colonies. If he succeeded, the entire shape of the war might change. The American effort to stop him involved many commanders, many units, and much confusion, but Arnold was among the most energetic and effective officers in the field.

His relationship with General Horatio Gates, the formal American commander, was terrible. Gates was cautious, political, and very aware of credit. Arnold was aggressive, impatient, and unwilling to stay quiet when he thought an opportunity was being missed. They argued fiercely. Gates at one point stripped Arnold of field command.

Then the battle intensified, and Arnold rode into it anyway.

This act captures almost everything difficult and impressive about him. He defied authority, which in one sense is exactly what armies cannot permit. Yet he did so while charging toward danger and playing a decisive part in the American success. At the Battle of Bemis Heights on October 7, 1777, Arnold helped lead attacks that broke key British positions and pushed the campaign toward American victory.

He was wounded again, severely, in the same leg that had been injured at Quebec.

The American victory at Saratoga had enormous consequences. It helped persuade France that the American cause was worth supporting openly, and the French alliance changed the whole war. Arnold's role in that victory was so important that later generations at Saratoga erected the Boot Monument, a memorial honoring the wounded leg of the unnamed general whose actions helped win the battle. The monument does not name Arnold. That silence is itself one of the strangest memorials in American history.

It tells the truth and refuses the name.

This is one reason Arnold remains so compelling. His treason was real, but so was the earlier heroism. Americans did not invent his military talent in order to make the betrayal more dramatic. The betrayal was dramatic because the talent had been real.

After Saratoga, however, Arnold did not move toward peace with his country. He moved deeper into anger.

Some of his bitterness came from physical pain. His leg injuries left him with lasting disability. For a man whose confidence and identity were tied to action, that mattered deeply. Some of the bitterness came from money. Arnold had spent heavily, lived expensively, and often felt that Congress or the states failed to reimburse him properly. Some of it came from politics. He believed enemies were circling him with accusations and delays.

And some came from personality. Arnold wanted appreciation in a way that steady men such as Washington did not. Washington wanted honor too, but he had greater ability to absorb insult without letting it direct his whole life. Arnold did not.

In 1778 he was appointed military commander of Philadelphia after the British evacuated the city. There he entered a different world from battlefield command: urban politics, trade disputes, social life, and accusations of corruption. Arnold mingled with prominent families, including loyalist-leaning circles. He married Peggy Shippen in 1779, and that marriage mattered greatly. Peggy moved in social and emotional proximity to the British world in ways Arnold increasingly did as well.

Charges of misconduct followed him. He demanded a court-martial to clear his name, and though he was not ruined by the verdict, the process humiliated him further. Washington, who still valued Arnold and understood his service, treated him with more generosity than many others would have. But even Washington could not repair Arnold's mind.

This is where the story becomes tragic in the older sense. Arnold had everything that might have fixed him in patriotic memory: battlefield courage, strategic importance, serious wounds, and the personal attention of Washington himself. Yet none of it satisfied him. He measured his life not only by what he had done, but by whether others admitted its value exactly as he thought they should.

That hunger grew poisonous.

By 1779 Arnold had begun secret communication with the British. The man who had helped save the American cause at Saratoga was now exploring its betrayal. His earlier heroism did not disappear. It became part of the darkness, because it showed what kind of man had now turned.

The leg wounded at Saratoga became a monument.

The man attached to it became the republic's most famous traitor.
