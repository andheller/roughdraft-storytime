# Chapter 4: West Point and the Price of Betrayal

By 1780 Benedict Arnold had crossed a line inside himself before he crossed it openly before the world.

He was in secret contact with the British, communicating through channels that eventually linked him with Major John Andre. Arnold's motives were mixed, and that matters. He wanted money. He wanted revenge for what he believed were humiliations and injustices. He wanted secure status in a world where he had come to feel dishonored by the Americans. He may also have convinced himself that the British cause was less hopeless than the American one, though revenge and ambition seem stronger forces in the record than sudden political conversion.

Arnold's most dangerous opportunity came when he obtained command of West Point.

West Point, on the Hudson River, was one of the most strategically important American posts in the war. Whoever controlled it could influence movement up and down the river and the larger communications of the northern theater. If the British seized it through treachery, the blow to the American cause would be severe.

Arnold offered exactly that.

He arranged to surrender West Point to the British for money and secure treatment. The plot required plans, timing, and a meeting with Andre, the British officer who served as the key go-between. Andre traveled behind American lines, met with Arnold, and carried papers connected with the scheme.

Then the conspiracy began to unravel.

Andre was captured by American militiamen in September 1780 while trying to return to British lines. The incriminating papers were found hidden in his boot. Once the evidence reached American authorities, the truth moved fast. Arnold, warned in time, fled downriver to a British ship before he could be arrested.

Andre was tried as a spy and hanged. His fate added an additional layer of bitterness to the story because Andre was widely regarded, even by enemies, as brave and honorable in his own way. Arnold escaped. The intermediary died.

This moment fixed Arnold's public image forever.

Everything he had done earlier now had to compete with a betrayal aimed at one of the republic's most valuable military positions. Americans who had praised him now cursed him. British officers accepted his utility but did not all admire him. Traitors are useful, but they are rarely fully trusted by those they join. Arnold discovered that betrayal can solve one grievance and create another kind of exile.

He received money and a commission in the British Army, but not the kind of secure glory he had once imagined. To Americans, he was infamous. To many Britons, he was useful but compromised. The heroic stature he wanted from one side could not be restored by the other.

Arnold then took up arms against the country he had once served. In 1781 he led British raids in Virginia and later in his home colony of Connecticut. The attack on New London and Groton Heights was especially hated. Burning, bloodshed, and the killing of American defenders deepened the loathing attached to his name.

This is the point where some readers may want a simple explanation: that Arnold had always been corrupt, or always selfish, or always false. But the historical record is more difficult than that. He had once risked everything for the patriot cause. He had once been wounded nearly to ruin while helping save it. The betrayal did not expose a fake hero. It exposed a real hero who had turned into something else.

That is why Arnold matters beyond his own life. He forces readers to ask how character breaks. Was the break already there in his pride and anger? Did repeated slights open it wider? Did physical pain, debt, and the politics of Philadelphia poison his judgment? The answer is not one thing. It is that several corrosive forces found a man already built in a dangerous shape.

George Washington handled Arnold's fall with remarkable seriousness. He had trusted Arnold and understood how valuable he had once been. The betrayal was not just a military threat. It was personal. Yet Washington also acted with the larger restraint that so often distinguished him. He focused on securing the army and seeing the crisis through rather than turning public anger into theatrical revenge.

Arnold lived on, but his reputation did not recover. Even in Britain he never became fully comfortable or fully honored. He spent later years in England after business disappointments and unease. He died in London in 1801, far from the Connecticut world that had made him and from the American cause that had once lifted him high.

The plot at West Point failed.

Its moral effect did not fail at all.

From then on, the name Benedict Arnold would serve Americans as a warning: courage without steadiness, talent without trustworthiness, and grievance allowed to rot into treason.
