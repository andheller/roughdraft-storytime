# Chapter 3: Why the Disaster Lasted

The event did not end when the most famous scene was over. Ship strikes an iceberg at 11:40 p.m. Lifeboat launch is slow and uneven. Titanic sinks at about 2:20 a.m.; rescue ship Carpathia arrives later.

Researchers still handle the details carefully. They often re-check death toll is usually given as more than 1,500, but exact totals depend on passenger lists and counting conventions and lifeboat capacity vs people aboard should be checked against final source choice. Historians usually agree on the broad shape of the event while still debating totals, categories, or which figures best describe what happened.

Memory also shapes this story. "Unsinkable" was part of publicity culture, but not always literally a formal company claim in the way later memory suggests. Class and gender strongly shaped survival rates.

The Sinking of the Titanic mattered because it changed more than the people who stood closest to it. Maritime prestige, overconfidence in technology, and lethal safety shortfalls. Disaster prompts major changes in sea safety rules. That is why the event remains worth studying long after the original fear, smoke, or confusion have passed.
