# Chapter 3: What the Riots Exposed

The event did not end when the most famous scene was over. Violence turns into racist terror targeting Black residents, homes, and businesses. The Colored Orphan Asylum is burned. Troops and police gradually suppress the riots.

Researchers still handle the details carefully. They often re-check death toll is disputed and may be far higher than older official counts and property damage estimates vary. Historians usually agree on the broad shape of the event while still debating totals, categories, or which figures best describe what happened.

Memory also shapes this story. This was not only anti-draft unrest; it became racial pogrom violence. The $300 commutation issue needs clear explanation without oversimplifying class resentment.

The New York City Draft Riots mattered because it changed more than the people who stood closest to it. Northern home front fractures under class anger, racism, and war fatigue. Shows that the Civil War was not politically settled even in Union strongholds. That is why the event remains worth studying long after the original fear, smoke, or confusion have passed.
