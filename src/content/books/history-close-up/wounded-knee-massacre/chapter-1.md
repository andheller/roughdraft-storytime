# Chapter 1: Fear on the Plains

The Wounded Knee Massacre is usually remembered for its most dramatic moment, but the story began earlier. Historians usually date the event to December 29, 1890. It unfolded in Near Wounded Knee Creek, Pine Ridge Reservation, South Dakota. By the time it reached the point most readers remember, pressure had already been building through fear, planning, rivalry, miscalculation, or stubborn political will.

Among the central figures were Spotted Elk (Big Foot), U.S. 7th Cavalry, Lakota men, women, and children. Tension rises around the Ghost Dance movement and U.S. fears of uprising. Big Foot's band is intercepted and brought to camp near Wounded Knee.

What made the buildup dangerous was the size of the stakes. Violent culmination of U.S. conquest policy on the Plains. A massacre later framed for years in official language as a battle.

Anyone watching closely before the decisive turn would have noticed winter camp ringed by troops, women and children running toward ravines under shelling, and snow-covered field after the shooting ends. Those details matter because they show the world before the break, when the outcome was still uncertain but the crisis was already hardening into something larger.
