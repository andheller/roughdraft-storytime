# Chapter 3: What the Massacre Meant

The event did not end when the most famous scene was over. Soldiers attempt to disarm the Lakota. A shot is fired amid confusion. Troops open sustained fire, including Hotchkiss guns, killing large numbers of Lakota, many unarmed.

Researchers still handle the details carefully. They often re-check lakota dead often listed as 150 to 300 depending on source and number of U.S. dead includes friendly fire and confusion. Historians usually agree on the broad shape of the event while still debating totals, categories, or which figures best describe what happened.

Memory also shapes this story. Use "massacre" rather than neutral battle framing unless explaining the older official label. Center Lakota experience and memory, not just army reports.

The Wounded Knee Massacre mattered because it changed more than the people who stood closest to it. Violent culmination of U.S. conquest policy on the Plains. A massacre later framed for years in official language as a battle. That is why the event remains worth studying long after the original fear, smoke, or confusion have passed.
