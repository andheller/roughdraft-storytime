# Chapter 3: The Beachheads and Beyond

The event did not end when the most famous scene was over. Dawn amphibious landings hit five beaches. Omaha becomes especially deadly; some sectors move inland faster than others. By nightfall the Allies hold fragile beachheads.

Researchers still handle the details carefully. They often re-check allied troops landed on June 6 are commonly given at about 156,000 across the five beaches and airborne drops, allied aircraft and ships totals vary depending on whether one counts all supporting operations, and casualty totals for D-Day alone differ by source. Historians usually agree on the broad shape of the event while still debating totals, categories, or which figures best describe what happened.

Memory also shapes this story. D-Day refers to the opening day of the invasion, not the entire Battle of Normandy. Canadian, British, Free French, and many other Allied forces must not disappear behind U.S.-only framing. German defenses were formidable but unevenly manned.

The D-Day Landings mattered because it changed more than the people who stood closest to it. The Western Allies must return to continental Europe. Failure would delay liberation and strengthen Nazi Germany. That is why the event remains worth studying long after the original fear, smoke, or confusion have passed.
