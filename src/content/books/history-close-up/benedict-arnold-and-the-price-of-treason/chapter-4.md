# Chapter 4: West Point and the Name That Broke

The plot unraveled because of papers, suspicion, and one bad journey.

In September 1780, John Andre met with Benedict Arnold near the Hudson to finalize arrangements connected to West Point. When Andre headed back toward British lines, he carried documents showing the fort's defenses and other sensitive information. He was traveling in disguise and under circumstances that made him vulnerable if stopped.

He was stopped.

Three American militiamen caught Andre near Tarrytown and found the papers hidden in his boot. That discovery broke the conspiracy open. Once the documents were examined, the shape of the betrayal became clear. Arnold had been negotiating the surrender of West Point to the British.

The consequences were immediate.

Andre was arrested and later executed as a spy. Arnold, warned just in time, escaped down the Hudson to a British ship. He had destroyed his reputation forever, but he had saved his own life. Washington arrived expecting to meet Arnold and instead found treason.

Imagine the shock.

This was not the exposure of some minor informer at the edges of the war. This was Benedict Arnold, one of the Revolution's once-celebrated officers, the wounded fighter of Saratoga, the bold commander of Valcour Island and Quebec, now revealed as a man willing to sell one of the republic's most important posts for money and favor.

Washington responded with a mixture of anger, disappointment, and cold necessity. The plot had failed. West Point was safe. But the emotional damage ran deeper than the military one. Arnold's betrayal became proof that courage in battle does not guarantee steadiness in character.

The British rewarded Arnold with money and a commission as a brigadier general in their army. Yet even on the British side he never became fully trusted or admired. He was useful, but stained. People are often eager to hire a traitor while remaining uneasy in his company. If a man will betray one cause, why assume he will remain faithful forever to another?

Arnold took part in British operations against the Americans afterward, including destructive raids in Virginia and Connecticut. These actions made him even more hated in the United States. He had not merely switched sides in private conviction. He had become an active enemy to the country he had once helped defend.

His later life brought little peace. After the war he struggled for stable respect in Britain and Canada. He remained defensive, bitter, and keenly aware that his name had become a byword for betrayal. He died in London in 1801, far from the American battlefields where he had once seemed so indispensable.

So what should readers make of Benedict Arnold?

First, he should not be simplified into a cartoon villain who was always false from the start. That would make the story easier and less truthful. Arnold really did fight bravely for the American cause. He really did help achieve major successes. The early republic owed him genuine military debts before it came to hate him.

Second, his betrayal should not be excused by pointing to grievance alone. Many officers were underpaid, overlooked, or insulted. They did not try to sell West Point. Arnold's complaints were real enough, but his response to them was his own. He chose money, revenge, and self-importance over loyalty.

Finally, Arnold matters because he exposes a hard fact about revolutions and republics. Political causes are not endangered only by weak or cowardly men. Sometimes the most dangerous betrayal comes from people of real talent who decide that their own wounded pride outweighs the cause they once served.

That is why his name lasted in such a harsh way. Americans did not remember him simply because he switched sides. They remembered him because he had once been so much better than that.

History rarely produces a fall more dramatic.

Benedict Arnold rose by courage, speed, and battlefield nerve. He fell by resentment, secrecy, and the fatal belief that his personal worth had become more important than the country whose uniform he wore.

The price of that choice was not only military.

It was his name itself.
