# Chapter 3: The Grievance That Grew into Treason

Benedict Arnold did not wake up one morning and become a traitor in an instant.

His betrayal grew gradually, fed by injury, pride, debt, resentment, and opportunity.

After Saratoga, Arnold's military fame should have secured him a place among the most admired American officers. Instead he entered a more troubled period. He was given command of Philadelphia after the British evacuated the city in 1778. There he moved into a social world of wealth, politics, and display that exposed more of his weaknesses. He lived beyond his means. He mixed business with public office in ways that invited criticism. He married Peggy Shippen, a young woman from a prominent Philadelphia family with social ties more sympathetic to the British side than Arnold's earlier circle had been.

None of these facts alone prove treason.

Together, however, they show a man drifting away from the hard discipline that had once made him effective.

Arnold felt wronged by the American government. Congress had frustrated him. Rival officers had irritated him. Civilian investigations examined his conduct in Philadelphia, and although the outcomes were mixed rather than utterly damning, Arnold took the process as humiliation. George Washington, who still trusted Arnold more than many others did, handled him more gently than some critics wanted. But even Washington's support could not cure Arnold's bitterness.

This is where careful judgment matters. Arnold had some real complaints. The patriot government was often chaotic. It did not reliably pay or honor officers. It could be political and inconsistent. But grievance becomes dangerous when a person begins to see every frustration as proof that loyalty no longer deserves to bind him.

By 1779 Arnold had moved into secret contact with the British.

The key intermediary in these contacts was Major John Andre, the talented and well-connected British officer who served under General Henry Clinton. Arnold opened negotiations offering military information and eventually something even more valuable: West Point.

West Point, on the Hudson River, was one of the most important American fortifications in the war. Whoever controlled it could influence movement along the river and the wider strategic shape of the northern theater. If Arnold could deliver West Point to the British, the blow to the American cause would be enormous.

Why did he do it?

Money mattered. The British promised financial reward. Recognition mattered too. Arnold believed Britain might value him more than the Americans had. He also seems to have persuaded himself that the American cause was failing or at least no longer worthy of the sacrifices it demanded. Traitors often build moral stories around their choices so they do not have to admit that anger and self-interest have become their real masters.

Arnold accepted command of West Point in 1780.

That appointment came from Washington, who still trusted him enough to place him in charge of one of the republic's most important military positions. The irony is brutal. Washington, the commander most associated with constancy, entrusted a man whose constancy had already begun to rot from within.

Arnold set about weakening the post's readiness while corresponding secretly with the British. Plans were made. Meetings were arranged. Codes and false names entered the story. What had begun as complaint had become conspiracy.

This is the point at which Arnold stopped being merely a difficult, bitter officer and became something much more dangerous. He was not only withholding full effort from his own side. He was actively trying to sell it into disaster.

The man he dealt with, John Andre, has often attracted sympathy because he was cultivated, brave, and personally appealing. Yet Andre was a willing participant in a plot to cripple the American cause. The difference between the two men is not innocence and guilt. It is that Andre was serving his own country, while Arnold was selling out his.

Still, the plot depended on secrecy and luck.

Secrecy can fail.

Luck can break.

Arnold was about to discover how quickly both can collapse when war moves from private bargain back into public fact.
