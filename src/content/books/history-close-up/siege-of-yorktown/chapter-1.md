# Chapter 1: The Trap in Virginia

The Siege of Yorktown is usually remembered for its most dramatic moment, but the story began earlier. Historians usually date the event to September 28 to October 19, 1781. It unfolded in Yorktown, Virginia. By the time it reached the point most readers remember, pressure had already been building through fear, planning, rivalry, miscalculation, or stubborn political will.

Among the central figures were George Washington, Comte de Rochambeau, Charles Cornwallis, Admiral de Grasse, Alexander Hamilton. Allied American and French forces march south after deceiving British observers. French naval victory in the Chesapeake blocks major British relief.

What made the buildup dangerous was the size of the stakes. Opportunity to trap a major British field army. Successful joint allied operation may effectively end the war.

Anyone watching closely before the decisive turn would have noticed siege trenches creeping closer each night, french warships controlling the bay, and night assault on Redoubts 9 and 10 with unloaded muskets and bayonets. Those details matter because they show the world before the break, when the outcome was still uncertain but the crisis was already hardening into something larger.
