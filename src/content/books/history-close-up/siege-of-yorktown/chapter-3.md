# Chapter 3: The Surrender That Changed the War

The event did not end when the most famous scene was over. Siege lines tighten around Yorktown. Allied troops storm key redoubts. Cornwallis surrenders his army on October 19.

Researchers still handle the details carefully. They often re-check surrendered British and German troops usually given at a little over 7,000 and allied troop counts differ by whether militia and naval personnel are included. Historians usually agree on the broad shape of the event while still debating totals, categories, or which figures best describe what happened.

Memory also shapes this story. Yorktown did not instantly end all fighting, but it destroyed British political will. French military and naval roles are essential, not supporting decoration.

The Siege of Yorktown mattered because it changed more than the people who stood closest to it. Opportunity to trap a major British field army. Successful joint allied operation may effectively end the war. That is why the event remains worth studying long after the original fear, smoke, or confusion have passed.
