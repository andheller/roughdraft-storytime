# Chapter 1: A New Weapon for the Blockade War

The Hunley Attack on USS Housatonic is usually remembered for its most dramatic moment, but the story began earlier. Historians usually date the event to February 17, 1864. It unfolded in Off Charleston, South Carolina. By the time it reached the point most readers remember, pressure had already been building through fear, planning, rivalry, miscalculation, or stubborn political will.

Among the central figures were H. L. Hunley submarine crew, USS Housatonic crew, Confederate engineer-inventors connected to the submarine program. Confederate submarine H.L. Hunley approaches the blockading USS Housatonic at night. Hunley uses a spar torpedo attack.

What made the buildup dangerous was the size of the stakes. First successful combat sinking of an enemy ship by a submarine. Demonstrates both innovation and extreme danger in experimental naval warfare.

Anyone watching closely before the decisive turn would have noticed hand-cranked submarine moving low in black harbor water, watchstanders spotting an unfamiliar object too late, and explosion flash, ship listing, then the submarine never returning. Those details matter because they show the world before the break, when the outcome was still uncertain but the crisis was already hardening into something larger.
