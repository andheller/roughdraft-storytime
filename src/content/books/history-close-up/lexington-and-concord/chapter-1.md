# Chapter 1: The March to Concord

The battles of Lexington and Concord are usually remembered for its most dramatic moment, but the story began earlier. Historians usually date the event to April 19, 1775. It unfolded in Lexington, Concord, and the road back to Boston, Massachusetts. By the time it reached the point most readers remember, pressure had already been building through fear, planning, rivalry, miscalculation, or stubborn political will.

Among the central figures were John Parker, Francis Smith, John Pitcairn, colonial militia leaders, Paul Revere and other riders as warning network figures. British regulars march from Boston to seize military stores at Concord. Lexington Green confrontation produces the "shot heard round the world".

What made the buildup dangerous was the size of the stakes. The imperial crisis becomes open war. Local militia show they can fight regular troops in a distributed way.

Anyone watching closely before the decisive turn would have noticed midnight alarm riders crossing dark roads, militia on Lexington Green at dawn, and smoke over North Bridge and musket fire from behind walls and trees. Those details matter because they show the world before the break, when the outcome was still uncertain but the crisis was already hardening into something larger.
