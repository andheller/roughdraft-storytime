# Chapter 1: A Sky Britain Could Not Lose

The Battle of Britain is usually remembered for its most dramatic moment, but the story began earlier. Historians usually date the event to July 10, 1940 to October 31, 1940. The most famous climax, Battle of Britain Day, was September 15, 1940. It unfolded in Skies over southern England, the English Channel, and southeast Britain, especially around London and RAF airfields. By the time it reached the point most readers remember, pressure had already been building through fear, planning, rivalry, miscalculation, or stubborn political will.

Among the central figures were Hugh Dowding, Keith Park, Winston Churchill, Hermann Goring, RAF fighter pilots known as "the Few". France falls in June 1940, leaving Britain exposed. Germany begins attacks on shipping and coastal targets.

What made the buildup dangerous was the size of the stakes. Britain must avoid defeat or invasion after the fall of France. If Fighter Command breaks, Germany may gain the air superiority needed for Operation Sea Lion.

Anyone watching closely before the decisive turn would have noticed pilots scrambling from grass airfields in minutes, ground observers and radar stations feeding a control-room map, and contrails, burning fuel, and parachutes over Kent. Those details matter because they show the world before the break, when the outcome was still uncertain but the crisis was already hardening into something larger.
