# Chapter 3: Why the RAF Held

The event did not end when the most famous scene was over. Luftwaffe pressure shifts toward RAF Fighter Command airfields and infrastructure in August. British radar, ground control, and fighter squadrons blunt the assault. German bombing shifts toward London and other cities in September, easing pressure on RAF bases. Hitler postpones invasion plans.

Researchers still handle the details carefully. They often re-check number of aircrew recognized as "the Few" is often given as 2,900-plus, aircraft losses vary by source, and civilian deaths are often folded into broader Blitz counts unless the source defines the battle narrowly. Historians usually agree on the broad shape of the event while still debating totals, categories, or which figures best describe what happened.

Memory also shapes this story. The battle and the Blitz overlap but are not identical. Avoid treating it as only Spitfires vs Messerschmitts; Hurricanes bore much of the load. National myth often compresses a long campaign into one shining day.

The Battle of Britain mattered because it changed more than the people who stood closest to it. Britain must avoid defeat or invasion after the fall of France. If Fighter Command breaks, Germany may gain the air superiority needed for Operation Sea Lion. That is why the event remains worth studying long after the original fear, smoke, or confusion have passed.
