# Chapter 3: The Long March South

In the summer of 1781, Washington and Rochambeau moved their combined forces with remarkable secrecy and discipline.

At first many observers thought they still intended to attack New York. That misunderstanding helped the allies. Then, once the real objective became clear, American and French troops began the long movement south toward Virginia.

This was one of the most impressive operational marches of the war.

Rochambeau brought professional order to the advance. French troops had to cross states, keep formation, manage supplies, and avoid fraying the alliance through bad behavior. Washington had to coordinate American needs with French capability. Roads, ferries, wagons, and local support all mattered. A campaign can fail without losing a battle if its movement breaks down. This one did not.

The march also displayed a rare kind of leadership. Washington and Rochambeau had to share credit while preserving clarity of command. Neither could indulge vanity too much. The purpose was not to decide which nation looked grander. The purpose was to trap Cornwallis before the British navy could rescue him.

At sea, Admiral de Grasse was just as important. His fleet entered the Chesapeake and helped shut the door. When a British fleet failed to reopen that door in the Battle of the Chesapeake, the campaign's center of gravity shifted decisively. Cornwallis, dug in at Yorktown, was suddenly in greater danger than many British leaders had expected.

The allied armies arrived and joined forces already operating in Virginia under Lafayette. Here the Revolution stopped looking like a scattered rebellion and began to look like a coordinated coalition capable of major siege warfare.

That change mattered. Yorktown was not won by inspirational speeches alone. It was won by engineering, artillery, trench work, fleet coordination, and the discipline to tighten a noose step by step.

Rochambeau understood this kind of war very well. His European experience made him valuable not because America needed aristocratic ceremony, but because siege warfare demanded method. He helped direct a campaign in which logistics, timing, and cooperation mattered more than theatrics.

By September 1781, Cornwallis was boxed in. The allies advanced their lines and prepared to force the issue. American troops and French troops both played critical roles in the attacks on key British redoubts. Each success made British survival less likely.

This was the road Rochambeau had spent a year preparing to walk.

It had begun in patience and ended in pressure.
