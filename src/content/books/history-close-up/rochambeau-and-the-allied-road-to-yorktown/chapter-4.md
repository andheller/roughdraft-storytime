# Chapter 4: Yorktown and the Meaning of Alliance

On October 19, 1781, Lord Cornwallis's army surrendered at Yorktown.

The war did not end that same afternoon, but British hopes of crushing the American Revolution suffered a fatal blow. Yorktown became the turning point that made peace possible.

Rochambeau stood at the center of that result.

He had helped bring a French army safely across the Atlantic, kept it intact through a long delay, coordinated with Washington without tearing the alliance apart, marched south in time, and contributed to a siege that trapped a major British force. These are not small achievements. They are exactly the kind that win wars.

Yet Rochambeau's story is often less famous than it deserves to be.

Part of the reason is national memory. Americans naturally prefer stories in which Americans appear entirely self-made. French memory, meanwhile, would soon be overtaken by the storms of the French Revolution and the rise of Napoleon. Rochambeau survived those later upheavals only narrowly. He served in revolutionary France, was arrested during the Reign of Terror, narrowly escaped the guillotine, and later lived under Napoleon's rule.

His life therefore connects two revolutions, though in a very different way from Lafayette. Lafayette became a symbol. Rochambeau remained what he had always been: a professional soldier trying to serve the state he belonged to while navigating violent change.

For students of the American Revolution, his most important lesson is clear.

Alliances matter.

The United States did not simply will independence into existence. It required foreign loans, foreign fleets, foreign soldiers, and commanders willing to cooperate across language and national pride. Rochambeau represents that reality in human form. He makes the Revolution look less like a lone miracle and more like a hard international victory.

That does not reduce George Washington's importance. In some ways it sharpens it. Washington's greatness included the ability to work effectively with an ally whose troops, experience, and money were indispensable. Rochambeau's greatness lay in answering that partnership with competence rather than rivalry.

The road to Yorktown was therefore more than a march. It was a proof.

It proved that republicans and monarchists, Americans and Frenchmen, volunteers and regulars, could cooperate long enough to defeat an empire stronger than either force alone. Such coalitions are fragile. In 1781 this one held.

That is why Rochambeau still deserves a place on the shelf.

He reminds us that history is often won not only by the most famous figure in the room, but by the ally who makes the room hold together.
