# Chapter 1: Wooden Fleets Meet Iron

The Battle of Hampton Roads is usually remembered for its most dramatic moment, but the story began earlier. Historians usually date the event to March 8 to 9, 1862. It unfolded in Hampton Roads, Virginia. By the time it reached the point most readers remember, pressure had already been building through fear, planning, rivalry, miscalculation, or stubborn political will.

Among the central figures were John L. Worden, Franklin Buchanan, John Ericsson, Confederate ironclad CSS Virginia, Union ironclad USS Monitor. CSS Virginia sorties on March 8 and devastates wooden Union warships. USS Monitor arrives overnight.

What made the buildup dangerous was the size of the stakes. First famous duel between ironclad warships. Signals the obsolescence of major wooden battle fleets.

Anyone watching closely before the decisive turn would have noticed shot clanging off armor rather than splintering wood, low freeboard Monitor nearly disappearing into smoke and spray, and shock among observers as familiar naval rules stop applying. Those details matter because they show the world before the break, when the outcome was still uncertain but the crisis was already hardening into something larger.
