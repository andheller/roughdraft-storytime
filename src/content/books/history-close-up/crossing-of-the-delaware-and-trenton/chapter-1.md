# Chapter 1: A Revolution Near Collapse

The Crossing of the Delaware and Battle of Trenton is usually remembered for its most dramatic moment, but the story began earlier. Historians usually date the event to Night of December 25 to 26, 1776; Battle of Trenton on December 26, 1776. It unfolded in Delaware River crossing from Pennsylvania into New Jersey; Trenton, New Jersey. By the time it reached the point most readers remember, pressure had already been building through fear, planning, rivalry, miscalculation, or stubborn political will.

Among the central figures were George Washington, Johann Rall, Henry Knox, John Glover. Washington's army faces collapsing morale and expiring enlistments. Troops cross icy river in a winter storm with artillery.

What made the buildup dangerous was the size of the stakes. The army needs a morale and recruitment-saving success. A failed attack could destroy Washington's force.

Anyone watching closely before the decisive turn would have noticed ice-choked black river at night, soldiers with frozen feet and wet muskets, and artillery dragged onto slippery roads before dawn. Those details matter because they show the world before the break, when the outcome was still uncertain but the crisis was already hardening into something larger.
