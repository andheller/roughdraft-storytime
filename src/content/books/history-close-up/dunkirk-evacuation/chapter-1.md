# Chapter 1: The Trap on the Coast

The Dunkirk Evacuation is usually remembered for its most dramatic moment, but the story began earlier. Historians usually date the event to May 26, 1940 to June 4, 1940. It unfolded in Dunkirk and nearby beaches on the northern coast of France; evacuation route across the English Channel to Britain. By the time it reached the point most readers remember, pressure had already been building through fear, planning, rivalry, miscalculation, or stubborn political will.

Among the central figures were Admiral Bertram Ramsay, Winston Churchill, British Expeditionary Force commanders, French defenders, civilian crews of the "little ships". German advance traps British and French troops near Dunkirk. Operation Dynamo begins from tunnels under Dover Castle.

What made the buildup dangerous was the size of the stakes. Britain risks losing the core of its trained army. A successful evacuation preserves manpower but not equipment.

Anyone watching closely before the decisive turn would have noticed long lines of soldiers standing waist-deep in surf, destroyers offshore, smoke, burning oil, and strafing aircraft, and the East Mole packed with men boarding under fire. Those details matter because they show the world before the break, when the outcome was still uncertain but the crisis was already hardening into something larger.
