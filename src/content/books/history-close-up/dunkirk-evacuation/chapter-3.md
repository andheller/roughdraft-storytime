# Chapter 3: What Dunkirk Saved

The event did not end when the most famous scene was over. Troops wait on exposed beaches and the harbor mole under repeated attack. Naval vessels, merchant craft, and civilian boats shuttle men off the shore. More than 300,000 Allied soldiers are evacuated before the perimeter collapses.

Researchers still handle the details carefully. They often re-check total evacuated is often given as 338,000-plus, including large numbers of French troops, number of "little ships" varies by counting method, and early estimates expected far fewer to escape. Historians usually agree on the broad shape of the event while still debating totals, categories, or which figures best describe what happened.

Memory also shapes this story. Dunkirk was both a military disaster and a logistical rescue. "Dunkirk spirit" can flatten the role of French troops who held the line and were also evacuated or captured. Equipment losses were enormous.

The Dunkirk Evacuation mattered because it changed more than the people who stood closest to it. Britain risks losing the core of its trained army. A successful evacuation preserves manpower but not equipment. That is why the event remains worth studying long after the original fear, smoke, or confusion have passed.
