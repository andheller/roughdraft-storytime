# Chapter 2: The Boats Under Fire

**DUNKIRK, FRANCE, May 31, 1940**  
On these beaches, where abandoned trucks, rifles, and helmets lie scattered in the sand, one fact matters above all others: the trapped Allied army is still getting out. Soldiers have spent hours and sometimes days waiting in the surf as bombs fall and machine-gun fire sweeps the shoreline. Yet lines of men continue to move toward small boats, harbor piers, and larger ships waiting offshore.

Operation Dynamo, directed from Dover, has become a rescue on a scale few expected when German forces cut off British and French troops in northern France. Destroyers, merchant vessels, and an extraordinary collection of civilian craft have all joined the effort. The scene is not one of victory. It is one of salvage. Guns, vehicles, and stores are being left behind because men must come first.

The rescue has already saved far more troops than many leaders thought possible. French troops still holding parts of the perimeter have made that escape possible, though many remain in grave danger. If this effort succeeds, Britain may lose its equipment, but it will not lose the trained core of the army it needs to continue the war.
