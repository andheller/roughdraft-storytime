# Chapter 2: Becoming Robert Shurtliff

When Deborah Sampson entered military service as Robert Shurtliff, she did more than put on a uniform. She stepped into a public identity that had to survive close inspection, fatigue, danger, and the constant nearness of other men.

That was a remarkable challenge.

A soldier in the Continental Army did not live privately. He marched, ate, drilled, slept, and fought in company. Privacy was thin. Clothing wore out. Bodies broke down. Illness spread easily. The army was one of the least forgiving places in the eighteenth century for anyone trying to hide a secret under daily strain.

Yet Deborah succeeded long enough to prove that the disguise could work.

She enlisted from Massachusetts in 1782, when the war was moving toward its final phase but had not fully ended. Yorktown had already taken place in October 1781, but peace had not yet been formally secured, and military service remained real and dangerous. Deborah joined the 4th Massachusetts Regiment. Once enlisted, Robert Shurtliff was expected to behave exactly like any other soldier: drill, march, obey orders, and carry a musket.

This required careful control over speech, gesture, clothing, and habit. It also required luck. Historians can say with confidence that Deborah served in uniform and kept her identity hidden for a significant period. They cannot reconstruct every practical detail of how she managed each day, because the records do not preserve that level of ordinary survival. But the fact of the success remains impressive.

Service also brought Deborah into the real military life of the late Revolutionary War. The army in these years was not a storybook force marching from one grand victory to another. Soldiers faced long movements, uncertain supplies, disease, and dull stretches broken by sudden danger. Even after Yorktown, skirmishes and military operations continued. The war had slowed, not vanished.

Deborah's unit operated in the Hudson Valley region and elsewhere in the Northeast. At some point during her service, she saw combat or dangerous action in which she was wounded. Accounts differ in exact detail, as they often do with lives reconstructed later from memory and retelling. But the central fact is consistent: she suffered wounds while serving as a soldier and took extraordinary steps to keep the truth from being discovered.

One of the most famous episodes in her story involves a wound to her thigh. Rather than seek full medical treatment and risk exposure, Deborah is said to have removed a musket ball or bullet fragment herself, or at least to have managed the injury privately in a way that allowed her disguise to continue. Historians approach the exact wording of this episode carefully because later biographies sometimes dramatized her life. Even so, there is strong reason to believe she concealed wounds and avoided ordinary care out of fear that a surgeon would discover her sex.

That decision captures the special risk she carried. Male soldiers feared pain, punishment, and death. Deborah feared those things plus discovery. Every injury posed two dangers at once.

Her service was therefore not only patriotic or adventurous. It was physically exhausting in a distinct way. To remain Robert Shurtliff, she had to stay alert inside every routine of army life.

This part of her story also invites a deeper question about the army itself. How did other soldiers not know? The answer lies partly in the conditions of military service. Men were often dirty, exhausted, and focused on immediate necessities. Clothing was loose, medical understanding was limited, and many assumptions were left untested so long as a soldier performed his duties adequately. Deborah passed because she was disciplined and because institutions often see what they expect to see.

As Robert Shurtliff, she seems to have been regarded as competent enough to continue serving. That matters. She was not carried through the army as a curiosity. She met military demands well enough that the disguise held under real pressure.

For modern readers, this can tempt oversimplified celebration. It is worth resisting that. Deborah Sampson's service was brave, but it was also lonely. She had to guard herself continuously. She could not share the truth of her life with those around her. The very success of the disguise required emotional isolation.

Still, there was power in the achievement. Deborah had entered a sphere closed to women and functioned within it. She had demonstrated not only courage, but capability.

The problem was that concealment grows harder over time.

Sooner or later, sickness, injury, or ordinary human attention makes secrets visible.
