# Chapter 1: A Hard Childhood in Massachusetts

Deborah Sampson did not begin life in comfort, and that matters because hardship shaped almost every important choice she later made.

She was born in Massachusetts in December 1760, in the town of Plympton. The world she entered was rural, demanding, and unforgiving to families with too little money or too little stability. Deborah's father disappeared from the family while she was still young. Her mother, struggling to support several children, could not easily keep the household together. As happened to many poor children in eighteenth-century New England, Deborah was placed out to work in other households.

This was not a short adventure in useful labor. It was the kind of childhood that teaches endurance very early.

For years Deborah lived as an indentured servant in the home of Deacon Benjamin Thomas. She worked hard, received limited formal schooling, and learned the sort of practical skills that country life demanded: managing tasks, observing people, and carrying responsibility without much room for complaint. At the same time, she seems to have taught herself beyond what her circumstances naturally allowed. Accounts of her later life suggest that she read when she could and paid attention to the larger world around her.

That larger world was changing.

By the 1770s, Massachusetts had become one of the centers of resistance to British policy. Arguments over taxation, representation, and imperial control hardened into confrontation. News of Lexington and Concord, Bunker Hill, and the mobilization of militia spread through towns and farms. The Revolutionary War was not an abstract conflict heard only through rumor. It changed local economies, political loyalties, and household expectations.

For men, the war opened paths into military service. For women, the situation was different. Women kept farms, managed households, followed armies as camp followers in some cases, and felt the war's consequences constantly. But regular enlistment in the Continental Army was not open to them. Military service, in the formal sense, belonged to men.

Deborah Sampson grew up inside that restriction.

She was physically capable, unusually self-possessed, and not inclined to accept narrow expectations easily. Those traits matter because she did not simply drift into the identity for which she later became famous. She had to imagine that it was possible at all. To do that, she first had to see how much of social authority depended on clothing, paperwork, and assumption.

People in every age like to believe they can always tell who belongs in which role. History keeps proving otherwise. Eighteenth-century communities had strong ideas about gender, but they also moved quickly through habit. A person dressed correctly, carrying the right name, and acting with confidence could sometimes pass farther than cautious observers might expect.

Deborah likely learned that by watching people rather than by hearing anyone explain it. Rural society was strict, but it was not all-knowing. Much depended on what neighbors assumed.

By the time Deborah reached adulthood, the war had dragged on for years. The Continental Army needed soldiers. Enlistment bounties and recruiting pressure rose. Service was dangerous, but it also offered pay, clothing, movement, and a place within the public struggle of the age. For a woman with limited property and limited legal independence, ordinary life offered far fewer openings.

This is where careful history matters. Deborah Sampson should not be turned into a simple tale of someone who only wanted excitement. Adventure may have mattered to her. So may patriot feeling. But economics, opportunity, and personal independence mattered too. A woman in her position had reason to understand military service not only as danger, but as access to a world normally closed to her.

There may also have been experimentation before her successful enlistment. Some records suggest she tried disguising herself and perhaps attempted enlistment once before being detected. As with many details in her life, the documentation is not perfect. Historians must work carefully, weighing memoir, pension records, later storytelling, and what can be firmly supported.

What is certain is that Deborah Sampson eventually chose to cross a line most women of her era were forbidden to cross. She would cut her hair, bind her body, adopt male dress, take a male name, and enter the Continental Army.

That decision did not erase the reality of the world around her. It exposed it.

If she could pass, even temporarily, it meant that military identity depended not only on law, but on performance and recognition. It also meant that the army, so often described as a school of republican virtue, was more vulnerable to assumption than its officers realized.

Deborah had spent her early life being assigned tasks by other people. In adulthood, she began trying to assign herself a role the world was not prepared to grant.

The next step would require nerve, planning, and silence.

She would enlist under the name Robert Shurtliff, sometimes spelled Shurtleff in later accounts.
