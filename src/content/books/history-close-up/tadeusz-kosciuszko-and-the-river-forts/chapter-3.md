# Chapter 3: Building West Point

After Saratoga, the Hudson River remained one of the war's critical corridors.

If the British controlled it fully, they could divide the rebellious states and move men and supplies along a vital route. The Americans therefore needed a strong defensive point on the river, and West Point became that place.

Kosciuszko played a central role in designing its defenses.

West Point was not chosen for beauty, though it had plenty of that. It was chosen because the river bent sharply there and the surrounding heights could be fortified. Kosciuszko helped design an interlocking defensive system of forts, batteries, redoubts, and obstacles intended to make British passage extremely difficult. The famous chain stretched across the Hudson formed part of this strategy, but the chain alone was not the fortress. Its effectiveness depended on guns, elevations, supporting works, and the relationship between the river and the surrounding hills.

This was demanding work.

To build a strong post, an engineer had to think about timber, stone, labor, weather, transport, and the arc of enemy artillery. He also had to imagine what an attacking commander would try first, and then design against it. Kosciuszko did this over time, shaping West Point into the most important American military position on the Hudson.

That importance later helps explain why Benedict Arnold's attempt to betray West Point to the British was so serious. A post worth betraying is a post of great value. Kosciuszko's work had helped make it so.

He did not labor alone. Soldiers, laborers, and other officers all contributed to the building and maintenance of the site. Yet the design intelligence behind the defenses was largely his. He thought in systems. One fortification supported another. One elevation protected a weakness elsewhere. One obstacle mattered because of the fire it enabled from above.

This is military engineering at its best: not isolated walls, but an organized landscape.

Kosciuszko also formed close human relationships during his American years. One of the most important was with Agrippa Hull, a free Black man from Massachusetts who served as his orderly. Their friendship is part of the moral arc of Kosciuszko's life. Living in a revolution that spoke of liberty while tolerating slavery sharpened his awareness of the distance between principle and practice.

That awareness did not remain abstract.

By the time the war ended, Kosciuszko had earned respect as one of the Continental Army's most capable engineers. He left behind forts, positions, and strategic advantages that outlasted the campaigns in which they were built.

But his deeper legacy would not be only technical.

He had come to America to serve liberty, and he was leaving with a clearer sense of how incomplete liberty could be.
