# Chapter 1: The Last Capital of Byzantium

The Fall of Constantinople is usually remembered for its most dramatic moment, but the story began earlier. Historians usually date the event to April 6, 1453 to May 29, 1453. It unfolded in Constantinople, capital of the Byzantine Empire, now Istanbul. By the time it reached the point most readers remember, pressure had already been building through fear, planning, rivalry, miscalculation, or stubborn political will.

Among the central figures were Mehmed II, Constantine XI Palaiologos, Giovanni Giustiniani. Ottoman forces encircle the city by land and sea. Heavy artillery batters the Theodosian Walls.

What made the buildup dangerous was the size of the stakes. End of the Byzantine Empire. Ottoman consolidation of a major imperial capital.

Anyone watching closely before the decisive turn would have noticed giant bombards firing against ancient walls, priests and civilians in crowded churches, and chains across the harbor and ships dragged over greased timbers. Those details matter because they show the world before the break, when the outcome was still uncertain but the crisis was already hardening into something larger.
