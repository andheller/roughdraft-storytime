# Chapter 3: The City That Changed Hands

The event did not end when the most famous scene was over. Defenders fight repeated assaults while trying to hold breaches. Ottoman ships are hauled overland into the Golden Horn, pressuring the city from another angle. Final assault on May 29 breaks through; the emperor dies in the fighting.

Researchers still handle the details carefully. They often re-check size of Ottoman army and fleet varies sharply by source, estimates of defenders are also inconsistent, and accounts of the final breach sometimes differ in detail. Historians usually agree on the broad shape of the event while still debating totals, categories, or which figures best describe what happened.

Memory also shapes this story. Many later tellings are shaped by Christian and Ottoman triumphalist memory. "End of the Middle Ages" is a textbook simplification, not a literal universal turning point.

The Fall of Constantinople mattered because it changed more than the people who stood closest to it. End of the Byzantine Empire. Ottoman consolidation of a major imperial capital. That is why the event remains worth studying long after the original fear, smoke, or confusion have passed.
