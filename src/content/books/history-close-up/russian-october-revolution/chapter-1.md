# Chapter 1: Russia on the Edge

The Russian October Revolution is usually remembered for its most dramatic moment, but the story began earlier. Historians usually date the event to October 24 to 25, 1917 on the Julian calendar; November 6 to 7, 1917 on the Gregorian calendar. It unfolded in Petrograd, Russia, especially the Winter Palace and key transport and communications points. By the time it reached the point most readers remember, pressure had already been building through fear, planning, rivalry, miscalculation, or stubborn political will.

Among the central figures were Vladimir Lenin, Leon Trotsky, Alexander Kerensky, Bolshevik Red Guards, soldiers and sailors in Petrograd. Provisional Government loses authority amid war, shortages, and political radicalization. Bolsheviks gain influence in soviets and military committees.

What made the buildup dangerous was the size of the stakes. Destruction of the fragile post-tsar political order. Beginning of Bolshevik rule and the path toward civil war and Soviet state formation.

Anyone watching closely before the decisive turn would have noticed armed workers and soldiers taking city nodes at night, smolny Institute buzzing as a revolutionary headquarters, and winter Palace entered amid confusion more than cinematic grandeur. Those details matter because they show the world before the break, when the outcome was still uncertain but the crisis was already hardening into something larger.
