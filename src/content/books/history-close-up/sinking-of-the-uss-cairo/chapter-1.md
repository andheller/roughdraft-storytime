# Chapter 1: War on the Western Rivers

The Sinking of the USS Cairo is usually remembered for its most dramatic moment, but the story began earlier. Historians usually date the event to December 12, 1862. It unfolded in Yazoo River, Mississippi. By the time it reached the point most readers remember, pressure had already been building through fear, planning, rivalry, miscalculation, or stubborn political will.

Among the central figures were Union gunboat USS Cairo, Confederate torpedo operators defending the river approaches to Vicksburg. Union naval forces probe river routes during the Vicksburg campaign. Confederate defenders place electrically detonated mines, then usually called torpedoes.

What made the buildup dangerous was the size of the stakes. Early example of mine warfare changing inland naval operations. Shows how vulnerable even armored river gunboats could be.

Anyone watching closely before the decisive turn would have noticed narrow river, hidden charges, and a sudden upward blast and crew scrambling off a sinking ironclad in cold water and mud. Those details matter because they show the world before the break, when the outcome was still uncertain but the crisis was already hardening into something larger.
