# Chapter 3: What the Sinking Revealed

The event did not end when the most famous scene was over. USS Cairo strikes two explosions and sinks quickly. Crew survives, but the ship is lost.

Researchers still handle the details carefully. They often re-check crew count and salvage timeline and whether Cairo was the first armored warship sunk by remotely detonated mines is often claimed and should be checked carefully in final draft. Historians usually agree on the broad shape of the event while still debating totals, categories, or which figures best describe what happened.

Memory also shapes this story. "Torpedo" in Civil War usage usually meant mine, not self-propelled torpedo.

The Sinking of the USS Cairo mattered because it changed more than the people who stood closest to it. Early example of mine warfare changing inland naval operations. Shows how vulnerable even armored river gunboats could be. That is why the event remains worth studying long after the original fear, smoke, or confusion have passed.
