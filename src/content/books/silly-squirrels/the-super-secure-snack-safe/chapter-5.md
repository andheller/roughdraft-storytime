# Chapter 5: Grandma Opens Everything

The next morning, Nutville looked like a party hosted by crackers.

The town square had pretzels in the fountain.

The mayor's statue wore a cookie around its neck.

Mrs. Rabbit found cinnamon acorns in her rain boots.

Mr. Mole charged two peanuts to remove "unauthorized snack rubble" from his tunnel entrance.

Papa woke up in the workshop chair with a cracker on his shoulder, flour in his fur, and no memory of anything after about sunset.

"Why am I wearing the trumpet?" he asked.

Because he was.

It was around his neck like a very confused necklace.

Nutty placed the evidence table in front of him.

On it sat:

- The empty safe
- The taped-over secret key slot
- A logbook from the safe
- Sixteen pretzels
- One note that read `MIDNIGHT COMFORT SNACK PROTOCOL ACTIVATED`

Papa read the note twice.

Then he looked up slowly.

"I would like to state for the record," he said, "that Sleepy Papa and Awake Papa have very different ideas about discipline."

Mama burst out laughing so hard she slid out of her chair.

Tony wiped tears from his eyes. "You built a machine that obeys your dreams."

Nutty flipped open the logbook.

"Worse," he said. "I accidentally trained the voice box on Papa while he was muttering about crackers."

He turned the pages toward the others.

"And then I connected the family setting, the conveyor belt, the voice box, and..." He squinted. "Oh no. The bedtime standby notes."

"The what notes?" Tony asked.

Nutty read aloud:

- `snack elevator?`
- `morale sandwich shelf`
- `family override`
- `tiny overnight cracker help for Papa maybe`

Mama clapped both paws over her mouth.

"I thought I was ignoring that last suggestion," Nutty said. "Apparently I was engineering it."

Papa studied the list and winced.

"In fairness," he said, "I may have requested several convenience features while hungry."

"Several?" said Mama.

"A modest number," Papa replied. "Somewhere between three and eleven."

"You asked for an overnight cracker setting?" Tony said.

Papa looked offended. "A tiny one. For emergencies. I did not ask it to achieve sentience and fire pretzels at the mayor."

Grandma tapped the safe. "And your seventeen security steps did exactly what overcomplicated security always does."

"What's that?" Nutty asked.

"It made the machine so busy protecting the snacks from everyone else that it forgot to protect the town from itself."

Nutty stared at the floorboards.

He hated it when Grandma was exactly right because she was always exactly right in such a calm voice.

"So what do we do now?" Papa asked. "I still want my snacks safe."

Grandma reached into her bag and set a plain wooden box on the table.

It had one sturdy lid.

One lock.

And one key.

That was all.

"This," she said, "is a snack box."

Everyone looked at it.

"That's it?" said Nutty.

"That's it."

"No horn?" Mama asked.

"No horn."

"No flour cannon?" Tony asked hopefully.

"No flour cannon."

"No goose question?" said Papa.

"Absolutely no goose question."

Papa considered the box with deep suspicion. "How will it know I am important?"

Grandma patted his shoulder. "It won't. That is part of its charm."

For one long second, nobody said anything.

Then Nutty started to laugh.

Not a tiny laugh.

A real laugh.

The kind that begins in your belly and bounces out before pride can stop it.

"We built a snack safe so secure it launched pretzels at the mayor," he said.

Mama snorted. "And interrogated Tony about socks."

"And called me crumb-fingers," Papa said, offended all over again.

Soon the whole workshop was roaring with laughter.

Even Mrs. Hedgehog smiled, though only with the left side of her face because that was apparently how she did wild joy.

Nutty wiped his eyes. "So the good news is, we did build a machine that can guard something."

"The bad news," said Papa, "is that the something cannot be snacks if I am asleep within shouting distance."

By afternoon, the Super Secure Snack Safe had a new job.

Mayor Buttersworth clasped his paws together. "At last! A place for Nutville's winter acorn fund that is not my desk drawer."

"Or your teapot," Mrs. Hedgehog said.

"Or your sash pocket," Tony added.

"We do not need the full list," said the mayor quickly.

They rolled it to the town square, polished off the flour, and painted new words on the front:

**NUTVILLE'S SUPER SECURE ACORN BANK**

The speaking tube still worked, so Nutty re-tuned the voice box.

Now, whenever someone dropped in a savings acorn, a rainy-day acorn, or a whole jar for the winter-help fund, the safe cheerfully announced:

"ACORN SAFELY BANKED. NO EXPLOSION DETECTED."

The town loved it.

Mrs. Hedgehog kept the ledger.

Mayor Buttersworth practiced saying "municipal acorn reserves" in a very deep official voice.

Papa loved the new snack box too, although he tried very hard not to show it.

It opened quickly.

It closed quietly.

And best of all, it did not throw crackers at anybody.

At dinner that night, he locked away a fresh batch of emergency cookies and slipped the key into his vest pocket.

"Simple," he said with a proud nod. "Elegant. Reliable."

Grandma sipped her tea.

"Where's the key now?" she asked.

Papa froze.

He checked his vest.

He checked his other vest.

He checked under his plate, inside the bread basket, behind his ear, and somehow in Mama's flower pot.

"...interesting," he said faintly.

Nutty leaned back in his chair, grinning.

"Should I design a key-finding machine?"

Everyone shouted, "NO!"

Then, from the pocket of Papa's trumpet necklace hanging by the door, came a tiny metallic clink.

The key.

Grandma closed her eyes.

"I am surrounded by professionals," she said.

Papa retrieved it, locked the snack box again, and patted the lid lovingly.

"This time," he said, "nothing can possibly go wrong."

From the town square outside, the repurposed safe cheerfully boomed:

"THIS IS AN ACORN BANK. TURNIP REJECTED."

Nutty's ears perked up.

Mama grinned.

Tony shouted from the road, "Who tried to bank a turnip?"

Papa looked at the trumpet necklace.

He looked at the snack box.

He looked at his family.

Then he sighed the sigh of a squirrel who knew peace would never last more than about six minutes in Nutville.

"Fine," he said, standing up. "But this time we are absolutely, definitely, one hundred percent not adding lasers."

Nutty quietly closed his notebook over a sketch labeled **Turnip Security Improvements**.

For now.

**THE END**
