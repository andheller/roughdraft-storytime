# Chapter 4: The Fountain Delivery Incident

The envelopes floated into the square like very confused snow.

Some landed on benches.
Some landed on hats.
One landed on the goose, which made the goose instantly furious at the mail as a concept.

But too many of them drifted toward the fountain.

Tony sprinted ahead with his delivery bag open wide.
Mama leaped across the cobblestones catching postcards in both paws.
Papa guarded the biggest bundle by sitting on it, which worked better than anyone expected.

Nutty scrambled onto the fountain edge and grabbed at a flapping bill just before it touched the water.
He got it.
Then he slipped.

He did not fall in.
He sat down abruptly in a flowerpot and looked so surprised that even Grandma laughed once through her nose.

Still, the situation was serious enough.
Important letters were drifting. Invitations were mixing with receipts. Somewhere in the confusion, the mayor's six speech invitations had all ended up in the duck pond, which Tony claimed improved them.

Grandma marched into the square with one basket under each arm and the expression of a squirrel who was done with decorative engineering.

"Everybody stop chasing the paper randomly," she said.

They froze.

"Line up by street. Catch only your own pile. Tony, bag the wet ones first. Nutty, turn the machine off before the post office mails itself."

This was a much better plan than chaos.

Within minutes the town had formed little sorting lines. Creek Road residents grabbed Creek Road letters. Orchard Hill neighbors passed postcards paw to paw. Tony stuffed rescued envelopes into dry sacks. Mrs. Hedgehog stood in the middle with a clipboard shouting addresses like a traffic officer for stationery.

And slowly, unbelievably, it worked.

The final envelope was plucked from the fountain rim by Mama, who landed beside Nutty and handed it over with a grin.

"There," she said. "Mail un-chaosed."

Nutty looked at the soggy corner, the mixed baskets, and the machine still rattling back in the post office.

"I may have overcomplicated the post," he admitted.

Grandma nodded. "You built a parade for envelopes."
