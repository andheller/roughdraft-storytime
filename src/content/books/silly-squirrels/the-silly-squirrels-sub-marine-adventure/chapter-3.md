# The Pine Cone Explorer

They worked all morning.

Papa opened up the pine cone scales to hollow out a cabin. Mama designed the air system: a network of hollow reeds that could telescope up to the surface. Nutty built paddle wheels twice the size of the acorn versions, plus a bark rudder and fins for steering.

The ballast system was the real improvement. Instead of pebbles on strings, Mama engineered waterproof pouches made from pine pitch and moss. Fill a pouch with river water, and the submarine sinks. Empty it, and the submarine rises. Precise, adjustable, and no string required.

Grandma sat in the workshop with her knitting. She did not offer advice. She did not say it would fail. She simply knitted and watched, which was somehow more unnerving than if she had been critical.

"You're being very quiet," Nutty said.

"I'm knitting."

"You're judging."

"I can do both."

By noon, the Pine Cone Explorer was finished. It had a proper hatch, three seats with moss cushions, sap windows for viewing, the telescoping reed air supply, the adjustable ballast pouches, paddle wheels, a rudder, and fins. Nutty had even carved a small compartment for emergency snacks, because Papa had asked eleven times.

"One compartment," Nutty said. "No conveyor belt. No midnight protocol."

"I was not going to suggest a midnight protocol," Papa said, in the voice of someone who had absolutely been about to suggest a midnight protocol.

---

Gerald was at the river. He had been there since dawn.

"Census," he said immediately.

Nobody responded to this.

The Pine Cone Explorer slid into the water and floated with a low, steady confidence that none of their previous submarines had possessed. It looked like it belonged in the river. It looked like it meant business.

Gerald circled it once. Twice. He poked it with a whisker.

"Adequate," he said.

"Thank you, Gerald."

"For a land mammal project."

Grandma settled on her rock with a fresh thermos and the same book she had not read yesterday. "Go on, then."

They climbed through the hatch. The cabin was roomy. All three fit with space to spare. The sap windows gave a greenish, wavering view of the river, and the light that came through was soft and strange and beautiful.

"Flood ballast," Nutty said.

Mama filled the first pouch. The submarine sank, smooth and slow.

Past the shallows. Past the middle water where the acorn fleet had turned back. Past the ledge where the bluegills lived and the watercress grew in angry little bunches.

"We're deeper than we've ever been," Papa said quietly.

Through the windows, the river was a different world. Underwater caves in the bank. Long trailing plants that swayed in the current. A crawdad the size of Nutty's fist, sitting on a rock and looking offended about it.

Gerald was swimming alongside them. He had stopped pretending he was doing anything else.

"The trench is ahead," he said. "Around the next bend, the riverbed drops off."

Mama checked the air reeds. All clear to the surface. She checked the ballast pouches. Plenty of capacity left. She checked the hull.

"Dry inside," she reported. "Not a single leak."

Nutty grinned. "Take us down."

---

The trench opened beneath them like a mouth.

The river, which had been wide and gentle, narrowed into a rocky corridor that dropped steeply into darkness. The light from above faded from green to gray to something close to black.

"More ballast," Nutty said.

Mama filled another pouch. They sank deeper.

Papa pressed his face to the sap window. "I can't see the bottom."

"There's a lot of river down here," Gerald said. He was swimming slowly now, looking around at rock formations he had never seen before. Forty-seven years on the river, and this was new to him.

"It's just mud down there, right?" Nutty said, with a small grin.

"Probably," Gerald said. But his voice had changed. The permanent grumpiness had thinned out, and underneath it was something that sounded a lot like wonder.

The Pine Cone Explorer descended into the deepest part of the trench. The walls were smooth stone, carved by centuries of current. Small fish with pale, almost transparent bodies drifted past the windows. A catfish twice Gerald's size glided by without a glance, heading somewhere important.

"How deep are we?" Papa whispered.

Nutty checked the depth gauge, which was a weighted string with knots tied at intervals. "Deeper than the string goes. I ran out of knots."

"That seems like something we should have planned better," Mama said.

"Noted for next time."

---

The bottom of the trench was not mud.

It was smooth stone, scattered with round pebbles and tufts of pale underwater moss. It was quiet down here. The current barely moved. The only sound was the soft creak of the pine cone hull and the gentle splash of the paddle wheels.

Nutty pressed his face to the window.

"There's nothing golden down here," he said. He didn't sound disappointed, exactly. More like someone confirming what he had already suspected.

Gerald drifted to the window. "Told you."

"You did."

"Forty-seven years I've been saying there's nothing magical at the bottom of this river."

"And you were right."

Gerald opened his mouth to say something grumpy, then closed it. He looked around at the smooth stone floor, the pale moss, the deep blue darkness stretching away in every direction.

"But I've never actually seen it," he said.

Nobody spoke for a moment.

"It's something, though," Papa said. "Even without a golden acorn. It's really something."

It was. The bottom of the river was strange and still and entirely unlike anything any of them had ever seen. Pale plants grew in clusters on the rocks. Tiny shrimp, almost invisible, drifted through the water like living dust. A snapping turtle the size of a dinner plate sat on a stone ledge and watched them with old, calm eyes.

"I have lived above this my entire life," Gerald said quietly, "and I never came down to look."

"Well," Nutty said, "now you have a submarine."

---

They surfaced in the late afternoon.

The Leaf Loft minnows cheered from their mailbox. The Bark Bed and Breakfast had a new sign out front: VACANCY - NO SQUIRRELS. The three acorn shells from yesterday were still beached on the bank. A family of clever fish had already connected two of them with sticks and were debating whether to add a patio.

"The Acorn Arms," Gerald said, looking at the fish condo. "Opening next week. You Nutwobbles have accidentally started an underwater housing boom."

Grandma was on her rock. Her book was open to the same page as yesterday.

"Well?" she said.

"We made it to the bottom," Nutty said.

"And?"

"It's mud and rocks and pale moss and a turtle that looked at us like we owed it money."

Grandma almost smiled. "No golden treasure? No magical discovery?"

"Nope."

"Good. Magical discoveries are very overrated." She poured Nutty a cup of tea from her thermos. "But you built a submarine that works. That's not nothing."

Papa climbed out of the hatch, stretched, and immediately went to check on the Bark Bed and Breakfast, which was fully booked and had a fish concierge.

"We did accidentally become the biggest real estate developers in the river," he said.

"Every failed submarine is somebody's dream home," Mama said.

Gerald surfaced near the bank. He looked at the Nutwobbles. He looked at the Pine Cone Explorer. He looked at the trench, somewhere far below, where the bottom of the river sat quietly being itself.

"So," he said. "Same time next week?"

Nutty stared at him. "You want to come back?"

"I did not say that." Gerald cleared his throat. "I said same time next week. That is a scheduling question, not an emotional one."

"We'll be here," Nutty said.

"Fine. I might also be here. Unrelated reasons."

"Census work?"

"Census work."

Gerald sank under the water. Then he came back up.

"The south fork," he said. "Past the big rocks. There's a whole canyon system down there I've never explored."

"Gerald," Mama said. "Are you asking us to take you exploring?"

"I am providing geographic information," Gerald said. "What you do with it is your business."

He sank again. This time he stayed down.

Nutty packed up his notebook. Inside were sketches of all three submarines, each one a little better than the last, each one with notes about what worked and what went wrong.

"You know what the best part was?" he said.

"The part where nothing exploded?" Grandma offered.

"The part where Gerald looked out the window and forgot to be grumpy."

Grandma folded her knitting into her bag. "That is a good part."

They walked home in the late afternoon light, leaving the Pine Cone Explorer anchored at the bank, ready for next week. Behind them, the Leaf Loft minnows were discussing an expansion. The Bark Bed and Breakfast had just received its first review, which was apparently four stars because the walls smelled like pine sap.

"South fork next week," Papa said. "We'll need a longer depth string."

"And more snack compartment," Papa added.

"One compartment," Nutty said.

"A slightly bigger one compartment?"

"We'll discuss it."

Grandma walked ahead, carrying her thermos and her unread book and the quiet satisfaction of a week that had gone exactly as badly and exactly as well as she had expected.

"Grandma," Nutty called. "Are you coming next week?"

She didn't turn around.

"Someone has to bring the towels," she said.

THE END
