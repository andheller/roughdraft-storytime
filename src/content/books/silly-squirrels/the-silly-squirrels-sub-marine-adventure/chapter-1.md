# The Leaf Sub and the Bark Sub

Nutty came to breakfast with a drawing.

The drawing showed a long tube with windows, a propeller, and three squirrels inside waving at a fish. Underneath, in Nutty's best handwriting, it said SUBMARINE.

"I would like to go to the bottom of the river," Nutty announced.

Papa Nutwobble set down his toast. "I have always wanted to go to the bottom of the river."

"You have never once mentioned the river," Mama Nutwobble said.

"I was waiting for the right moment." Papa studied the drawing. "This is the right moment."

Grandma Nutwobble was visiting for the week. She looked at the drawing over her tea. Then she looked at Nutty. Then she looked at Papa.

"No," she said.

"We haven't asked you anything yet," Nutty pointed out.

"I can see where this is going." Grandma sipped her tea. "I have been in this family a long time."

Mama picked up the drawing and turned it sideways. "What are you planning to build it out of?"

Nutty had not gotten that far.

---

By mid-morning, they had gathered what Nutty called "perfect submarine materials" from around the yard: autumn leaves, vines, and pine needles.

Papa held up a large red maple leaf. "Waterproof."

"That is not waterproof," Grandma said from the porch.

"It repels rain," Papa said.

"Rain lands on it. Then the leaf gets wet. That is the opposite of repelling."

Papa considered this. "It repels rain briefly."

They built the submarine anyway. It looked like a large, aggressive salad held together with vines. Nutty added pine needles for what he called "technical detail." Nobody asked what that meant.

"Beautiful," Papa said.

"Ambitious," Mama said.

"I'll be on the porch," Grandma said.

---

At the river, the leaf submarine floated for about four seconds.

Then it began soaking up water with the enthusiasm of a towel that had been waiting its whole life for this opportunity.

"It's drinking the river," Nutty observed.

The vines went slack. The pine needles drifted away. The whole thing sagged, darkened, and sank with a quiet, defeated gurgle.

Papa watched the last bubbles rise. "Hmm."

A face surfaced where the submarine had gone down. It belonged to a catfish named Gerald, who was old, gray, and had the permanent expression of someone standing in a long line at the post office.

A soggy leaf was draped over one whisker.

"Let me guess," Gerald said.

"We're building a submarine," Nutty said.

"You built a salad." Gerald removed the leaf with great care. "And you dropped it on my roof."

"We're going to explore the bottom of the river," Nutty continued, because he had not yet learned to read a room when the room was underwater.

Gerald stared at him for a long time.

"The bottom of the river," Gerald said, "is mud. I have lived in this mud for forty-seven years. It is not exciting mud. It is regular mud."

"There could be anything down there," Nutty said.

"There is mud down there. I just told you."

"We'll see," said Nutty.

"You will see mud," said Gerald, and sank back under the water.

---

That afternoon, Nutty had a new plan.

"Tree bark," he said. "Bark protects trees from rain. It's nature's waterproofing."

Mama nodded. "Better reasoning this time."

"Worse reasoning," Grandma said from the porch, "but louder."

They collected bark from fallen branches. Birch bark that curled like paper. Oak bark with ridges. Pine bark that smelled like the holidays. Nutty drew a real blueprint this time: a sealed hull, a hatch on top, sap for glue, and a periscope made from a hollow reed.

Papa was in charge of shaping the hull. Mama sealed the seams with pine sap. Nutty carved the periscope himself, which took two hours and looked like a reed that had been in a fight, but it worked.

"Much better," Mama said, stepping back.

It did look much better. It looked like something that might actually go underwater on purpose.

"Not bad," Grandma said. She had come down from the porch to inspect it, which was the closest Grandma got to a compliment.

---

At the river, the bark submarine floated. It kept floating. It floated with confidence.

"Get in before it changes its mind," Mama said.

All three squeezed through the hatch. It was tight. Papa's face was pressed against a piece of oak bark. Mama's tail was in Nutty's ear. But they were inside, and the submarine was holding.

Papa dropped the ballast stones. "Dive."

They sank. Slowly, smoothly, properly.

"I can see fish," Nutty whispered through the periscope. "And a turtle. And some kind of plant that looks angry."

"That's watercress," Mama said. "It always looks angry."

For three full minutes, everything was perfect.

Then Nutty noticed his feet were wet.

Then Mama noticed her feet were wet.

Then Papa noticed that everyone's feet were wet and the water was rising and the bark seams were leaking in about fourteen places at once.

"The sap isn't holding," Mama reported, which was obvious because the river was now ankle-deep inside the submarine.

Papa tried to reach the emergency ballast release. He could not move his arms because he was wedged between Mama and a curved piece of oak.

"I am stuck," Papa said calmly.

"We are all stuck," Mama said, less calmly.

"And sinking," Nutty added, for completeness.

---

Ten minutes later, three wet, bark-covered squirrels sat on the riverbank.

Gerald surfaced next to the half-sunken bark hull. He examined it the way a building inspector examines a house that has been built entirely out of optimism.

"Another one," Gerald said.

"We lasted ten minutes this time," Nutty said.

"Congratulations. Your leaf pile from yesterday? A family of minnows moved in. They're calling it the Leaf Loft. They put up curtains."

Mama blinked. "Seaweed curtains?"

"Little ones. Very tasteful." Gerald looked at the sinking bark hull. "I give this one three days before it becomes the Bark Bed and Breakfast. The local fish are already forming a waiting list for Nutwobble-brand disaster housing."

"We are not trying to build fish houses," Nutty said.

"And yet," Gerald said.

He sank back under the water.

On the porch at home, Grandma was waiting with towels and hot cocoa.

"How was the bottom of the river?" she asked.

"We didn't reach the bottom," Nutty admitted.

"I am astonished," Grandma said, in a voice that contained no astonishment whatsoever.

But Nutty was already sketching in his notebook. The leaves had been too absorbent. The bark had been too leaky. He needed something naturally waterproof, naturally sealed, naturally the right shape.

He looked at the acorn on the kitchen table.

He looked at it for a long time.

"Papa," he said. "How many giant acorn shells do you have in the workshop?"

Papa grinned. "Three."

Grandma sighed the sigh of a squirrel who could see exactly what was coming and knew there was no stopping it.

"More towels tomorrow, then," she said.
