# Chapter 3: Crossing with Extra Boing

Market morning in Nutville involved many baskets.

Berry baskets.
Bread baskets.
Flower baskets.
Mystery baskets that looked ordinary but turned out to be full of turnips wearing ribbons.

All of them had to cross Walnut Creek.

The first basket trouble began with Mrs. Pigeon's muffins.
She stepped onto the bridge, hit the spring planks, and the muffin basket bounced neatly out of her wings and landed on the railing.

"Rude," said Mrs. Pigeon.

Next came Mr. Mole with jars of pickles.
The bridge boinged.
The jars rattled so hard the pickles looked seasick.

Then came Papa, carrying a crate of cheese crackers for the town snack table.
The bridge gave a cheerful wobble.
Papa wobbled back.
The crackers rose into the air in a golden cloud of crunchy panic.

Nutty, Mama, and Tony dove in three different directions.
Mama caught the crate.
Tony caught Papa.
Nutty caught exactly one cracker and felt that history should remember this.

"We need a smoother crossing mode," Nutty said.

He crawled underneath the bridge with a wrench and added two side braces, one center strap, and what he described as a "de-boing limiter."

Grandma looked over the edge. "Does that make it less bouncy?"

Nutty paused.
"It makes it bouncy on purpose."

This was not the answer she wanted.

Still, the next few crossings improved.
A wheelbarrow went across with only one hop.
Two ducklings bounced so lightly they thought it was a game.
The mayor crossed carrying a watermelon and lost only his dignity.

Then Tony arrived with the largest delivery of the day: three stacked picnic baskets, a parcel of library books, and one jar of Aunt Tilly's Famous Jam.

"Clear the bridge!" he called.

Everyone held their breath.
Tony rolled carefully onto the planks.
The cart swayed.
The springs dipped.
The side ropes hummed.
The bridge gave one huge dramatic boing.

The cart lifted.
Not all the way.
Just enough to make every squirrel in Nutville yell the same word.

"NOPE!"

One picnic basket flew right.
One flew left.
The jar of jam spun overhead like a shiny red moon.
The library books slid forward in a neat stack and landed in Mama's paws.

The crowd scattered.
Nutty leaped.
Papa lunged.
Grandma did not leap at all. She simply reached up with one hand and caught the jam jar out of the air as if this sort of thing happened before breakfast every day.

Tony stared.
"Can you teach that?"

"No," said Grandma.

Nutty looked at the bridge. The bridge looked back in the way only a machine could, by trembling smugly.

"All right," he said. "This has become a basket emergency."
