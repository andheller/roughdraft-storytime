# Chapter 4: The Great Basket Rescue

The basket emergency reached its worst point at noon.

A delivery line had formed on one side of the creek.
A shopping line had formed on the other.
And in the middle stood the Bouncy Bridge, wobbling like it had just heard exciting news.

"We need to get these baskets across," said Mrs. Hedgehog. "Without launching lunch into the creek."

Nutty paced in circles.
Mama bounced once experimentally.
Papa arranged rescue blankets below the railings in case of airborne crackers.
Tony tightened the straps on his cart.

Then Nutty snapped his fingers.
"Counter-bounce!"

Everyone stared.

He pointed to the bridge.
"If one squirrel crosses alone, the bounce gets wild. But if two squirrels move together on opposite sides, the wiggle cancels! Maybe. Probably. It should, if the bridge respects symmetry."

Grandma narrowed her eyes.
"And does it?"

Nutty looked at the bridge.
The bridge made a small springy *boop*.

"We'll find out," he said.

The plan was simple.
Mama and Tony would cross at the same time, step for step, carrying equal baskets.
Papa would stand at the center as the official snack safety marshal.
Grandma would be nearby with a rope "in case geometry got ideas."

They began.
One step.
Two steps.
The bridge dipped.
Then steadied.
Three steps.
Four steps.
The planks flexed, but only politely.

The crowd gasped.

"It's working!" cried Nutty.

Halfway across, Papa sneezed.

This would not normally be important.
But Papa's sneeze caused him to bounce slightly, which caused Mama to over-correct, which caused Tony to squeak, which caused the bridge to remember it was still a ridiculous bridge.

BOOOING.

Up went one basket of bread rolls.
Out went one rope of onions.
Across flew Tony's hat.

But this time Nutville was ready.
Mama snatched the bread basket midair.
Tony caught the onions with his tail.
Papa trapped the runaway crackers under his elbow.
And Grandma yanked the safety rope once, pulling the whole wobble back into line.

The crowd erupted in cheers.

It was not graceful.
It was not elegant.
But by late afternoon every basket, wagon, jar, bundle, and loaf had crossed the creek.

Nutty sat on the bank panting.
"So," he said, "the bridge is useful."

Mrs. Hedgehog adjusted her glasses.
"The bridge is survivable. Let us not over-celebrate."

Grandma tapped the nearest spring with her boot.
"Tomorrow we remove two of these."

Nutty opened his mouth.
Then he looked at the jam-stained rope, the cracker blankets, the hat in the tree, and the mayor still dusting muffin crumbs from his sash.

"That seems fair," he admitted.
