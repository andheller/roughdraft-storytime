# Chapter 6: Idea Index and 2026 References

## Idea Index

If your budget is small:

- build on private land  
- run the micronation as a club, retreat, camp, or event space  
- focus on identity, ceremonies, signs, and membership

If your budget is medium to large:

- look at islands as property projects first and sovereignty dreams second  
- plan docks, water, power, waste, storms, and access before designing medals

If your budget is barge-shaped:

- consider a live-aboard vessel, floating clubhouse, or ceremonial capital  
- assume maintenance will become a personality trait

If your budget is "I know marine engineers":

- assume the offshore option is the hardest one, not the freest one  
- treat it like engineering plus law plus logistics, not like a loophole

If your heart wants a micronation but your wallet wants lunch:

- choose land  
- name it magnificently  
- build traditions people actually enjoy  
- survive long enough to become interesting

## 2026 Reference Notes for the Grown-Up

These are the main sources this guide was based on, checked on **March 24, 2026**:

1. **NOAA Office of Coast Survey, U.S. Maritime Limits & Boundaries**
   The NOAA page explains the 12 nautical mile territorial sea, 24 nautical mile contiguous zone, and 200 nautical mile EEZ, and notes that the coastal state exercises sovereignty in the territorial sea while having defined rights and jurisdiction in the EEZ.

2. **United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, Part V, Article 60**
   The U.N. text says coastal states have the exclusive right to authorize and regulate artificial islands and structures in the EEZ, and that such structures do not possess island status or a territorial sea of their own.

3. **United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, Part VII, including Articles 89, 91, and 92**
   The U.N. text states that no state may validly purport to subject any part of the high seas to its sovereignty, and that ships operate under the flag of a state.

4. **U.S. Coast Guard Boater's Guide to the Federal Requirements for Recreational Boats**
   Current Coast Guard boating guidance says recreational vessels with propulsion machinery must be registered in the state of principal use, and documented vessels may still face state requirements.

5. **U.S. Army Corps of Engineers Regulatory Guidance**
   Current USACE regulatory materials explain that structures or work in, over, or under navigable waters of the United States can require Section 10 permitting, including things like artificial islands, permanent mooring structures, and permanently moored floating vessels.

## Final Advice

The best micronation in 2026 is probably not the one shouting the loudest about sovereignty.

It is the one that:

- knows what land or vessel it actually controls  
- understands which law applies there  
- can afford maintenance  
- keeps people safe  
- and has a flag nice enough to make visitors say, "Honestly, this is ridiculous, but in a committed way."
