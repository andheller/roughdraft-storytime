# Chapter 4: Budget Tier Three: Barge Majesty

Perhaps land feels too ordinary.

Perhaps islands feel too expensive.

Perhaps you have looked at a barge and thought:

**What if my capital city floated?**

Now we are getting somewhere.

A houseboat, barge, or other live-aboard vessel can be a genuinely interesting micronation base. It can move. It can visit ports. It can host ceremonies on deck. It can have a cabin called the Hall of Parliament even if Parliament is just three folding chairs and a very damp clipboard.

This option is not silly.

It is only partly silly.

For the right founder, it may be the best mix of drama and possibility.

But the legal lesson for the grown-up is simple:

boats still come with systems.

In the United States, current Coast Guard boating guidance says recreational vessels with propulsion machinery must be registered in the state of principal use, and documented vessels may still have state-level fees or validation requirements depending on the state.

So the floating micronation is not:

**a loophole palace on water**

It is:

**a marine project with identity**

You can still do many excellent micronation things:

- choose a national flag  
- name the vessel grandly  
- define citizenship or membership  
- appoint officers  
- host "state visits" by docking somewhere else  
- write a constitution that includes the phrase "all muffins shall be divided fairly"

But you also inherit:

- maintenance  
- fuel or towing costs  
- hull problems  
- marina contracts  
- waste systems  
- weather risks  
- safety equipment  
- insurance arguments

The barge option is good for founders who want movement, ceremony, and a strong symbol.

It is bad for founders who hate maintenance, ropes, corrosion, pumps, paperwork, or the sentence "something smells wrong in the bilge."

In other words, this is the tier where your micronation becomes less like a map and more like a machine.

Machines are wonderful.

Machines also demand tribute.
