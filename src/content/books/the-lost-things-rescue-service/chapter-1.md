# Chapter 1: The Lost Things Rescue Service

Lost things never stay in the middle of a room for long.

They edge themselves toward corners. Under benches. Behind radiators. Into the skinny strip between the elevator and the wall where only dust and brave pennies like to go. I know this because I am the founder of the Lost Things Rescue Service in the Marigold Apartments, and also because I pay attention in a way that is nearly professional.

I started the service on a Saturday morning after finding one child-size rain boot beside the lobby ficus.

It was green with yellow frogs and looked so abandoned I had to sit down beside it for a minute.

"Someone will come back for it," my mother said. She was hurrying to work, pinning up her hair with a pen because she could never find a real clip when she needed one.

"That's what people always say about lost things," I told her. "And sometimes they don't."

She looked at the boot, then at me. "No charging money."

"It's a rescue service," I said. "Not a shoe ransom."

That made one side of her mouth smile, which I was glad to see because lately her smiles had been getting shorter. She kissed the top of my head, left smelling like coffee and hospital soap, and I dragged the folding card table from our hall closet into the lobby.

I made a sign on the back of an old cereal box:

**LOST THINGS RESCUE SERVICE**  
**OPEN UNTIL LUNCH OR EMERGENCY**

Under that, I put the green boot, a blue mitten I had found in January and never solved, three hair clips, a toy car missing one door, and a grocery list that said:

milk  
eggs  
dish soap  
DO NOT FORGET CILANTRO AGAIN

By nine-thirty I had my first customer.

Mrs. Gutierrez from 2B came down with curlers in and peered at my table. "Well," she said, "there's my list. No wonder I came home yesterday with peaches and no soap."

I handed it over. "You're welcome."

She squinted at the sign. "Rescue service?"

"Lost things get lonely," I said. "I return them before they get strange."

Mrs. Gutierrez laughed so hard one curler tilted sideways. She gave me a butterscotch from her pocket and told me I was a public good.

At ten o'clock I returned the boot to the little boy in 1A, who had been hopping around his apartment "like an uneven flamingo," according to his father. At ten-fifteen I reunited the toy car with the twins in 5C, though they argued for a full five minutes over whose grief had been greater. At ten-thirty I found out the blue mitten belonged to nobody in the building, which made it officially mysterious and therefore vice president.

The only thing wrong was that Tessa still hadn't come down.

Tessa lived in 4D and had been my best friend since kindergarten, when she bit a boy for calling my drawing of a horse "a melted dog." That is loyalty. We used to spend Saturdays doing important work together. Once we ran a hospital for injured leaves. Once we made a newspaper that reported only neighborhood emergencies, like Mr. Alvarez's escaped parakeet and the fact that the laundromat machine on Sycamore Street was eating socks again.

But lately Tessa had been acting like a person with a radio on in another room. She answered half a second late. She forgot plans we had definitely made. She kept saying "maybe" in a way that sounded like "no" had put on a sweater and glasses.

When she finally came into the lobby, she was carrying packing tape.

That by itself should have told me something, but my mind went right past it.

"Do you want to be investigations chief?" I asked.

She looked at the table. "For what?"

"For the service."

"Nell," she said, and when she says my name slowly like that, it means she is about to disappoint me in a careful manner. "I can't today."

"You can't all day?"

She tucked the tape under her arm. "Probably not."

"Probably is not an answer. It's a fog."

She almost smiled, then didn't. "I have stuff to do upstairs."

"More important than a public good?"

"Nell."

That was all. Just my name again, but flatter.

She turned and went back toward the stairs, and I had the very ugly feeling that something had happened without telling me first.

At eleven, Mr. Leone from 4B came down in his brown cardigan with the elbows gone shiny. He always smelled faintly like peppermints and old paper. He stood in front of my table for a long moment, looking not at the objects but at the sign.

"My Nell," he said finally, "do you take requests?"

"Important ones," I said.

He nodded and took a breath. "I'm missing a bird."

I looked at the lobby ceiling automatically.

He gave a short laugh. "A pin. Blue enamel. About this big." He held up his fingers. "My wife used to wear it on her coat. One wing is chipped."

I knew the bird. Mrs. Leone had died last winter, when the trees still had Christmas lights on them and everyone in the building started talking softer for a while. After that, Mr. Leone sometimes wore the pin on his own sweater, not because it matched, which it did not, but because grief makes different rules.

"When did you see it last?" I asked, because that is what investigators say.

"That," he said, "is where things become embarrassing."

I waited.

He looked over one shoulder, like embarrassment might be standing behind him. "I remember holding it. I remember thinking I should put it somewhere safe. Then I had the very good idea of making tea. Then I found myself watering the plant by the mailboxes. Somewhere in there, the bird departed."

"That is not embarrassing," I said. "That is just too many thoughts at one time."

His face changed a little at that, like a window opening a crack.

"Will you help me?" he asked.

I wrote **BLUE BIRD PIN, 4B, VERY IMPORTANT** in my investigation notebook.

"I will," I said.

So I searched.

I checked the mailboxes, the lobby plant, the radiator ledge, the elevator track, the bench by the front door, and the little brass dish on the table by Mr. Leone's apartment where people leave flyers and things they don't want to carry upstairs. No bird.

I went up to 4B to ask more questions, and that was when I saw the boxes outside 4D.

They were stacked against Tessa's door, three of them, with black marker on the sides.

**BOOKS**  
**KITCHEN**  
**TESSA ROOM**

I stared so hard my eyes started to sting.

Right then Tessa came out carrying a roll of bubble wrap.

I pointed. "What are those?"

She followed my finger. Her whole body got still.

"Boxes," she said.

"I can see that. Why do you have them?"

She pressed her lips together. "We're moving."

If someone had pushed me backward down the stairs, I could not have felt more surprised.

"What?"

"My mom found a place closer to my grandma. We're leaving next week."

"Next week?"

She nodded once.

"And you were not going to tell me?"

"I was."

"When? From the highway?"

"That is not fair."

"No, what is not fair is your whole entire apartment being in boxes and me finding out because cardboard tattled."

Tessa looked tired all of a sudden, older than me in the annoying way she sometimes did. "I didn't know how."

"So you chose not at all."

She dropped the bubble wrap on top of a box. "I chose not yet."

"That's the same thing if you're the person who doesn't know."

Her eyes got shiny, which only made me angrier, because it is inconvenient when the other person starts to look hurt too.

"My mom kept saying, 'We'll tell people when it's final,'" Tessa said. "And then it was final. And every time I thought about telling you, it felt like if I said it out loud the walls would start doing it faster."

I hated that sentence because I understood it.

So instead of saying anything kind, I said, "Fine," which is a rotten little word that means seventeen different bad things at once.

Then I went downstairs and shut down the Lost Things Rescue Service for lunch even though it was only eleven-forty-five.

I carried the table into the laundry room because it was cool there and smelled like detergent and wet cement, which matched my mood. I sat on an upside-down bucket beside the dryers and thought terrible thoughts about cardboard.

After a while my mother found me.

I hadn't heard her come home. She still had her work badge on and the tired bend in her shoulders.

"Mrs. Gutierrez said there had been a closure of public services," she said, sitting on the other bucket.

I looked at the floor.

"What happened?"

"Tessa is moving."

My mother let out a slow breath. "Ah."

"She knew and didn't tell me."

"Ah," my mother said again, only softer.

I picked at the peeling label on the detergent box beside me. "It's like being made into the last person in your own life."

"That is a very fierce sentence."

"It's true."

She was quiet for a moment. Then she said, "When I was nine, my best friend moved to Arizona and I was offended at the entire western half of the country."

I glanced at her.

"She did tell me," my mother said. "I still thought being angry might fix it."

"Did it?"

"No. But it gave me something to do with my hands."

That made me smile a little, against my wishes.

She nudged my sneaker with hers. "You can be mad," she said. "Just don't let being mad convince you that you've stopped loving someone. Those are different problems."

That was a very mother thing to say. I did not answer, because sometimes when a grown-up is exactly right, talking feels unnecessary and also rude.

Just then one of the donation bags by the wall tipped over with a soft thump. A gray wool coat slid halfway out.

I would not have noticed except something small and blue caught the light near the pocket.

I was off the bucket before my mother finished saying, "What is it?"

The coat had a tag safety-pinned inside the collar: **4B**.

My heart started beating in a professional way.

"I think," I said, "there has been a development."

The blue bird pin was tucked into the coat pocket lining, hooked through the fabric as neat as if someone had put it there on purpose and then forgotten their own excellent hiding place.

I stood very still.

"Mr. Leone's?" my mother asked.

I nodded.

Then I looked toward the ceiling, where Tessa's apartment was, one floor up and full of boxes.

"I need my investigations chief," I said.

My mother did not smile this time, but something in her face softened. "Go get her."

Tessa opened the door before I knocked twice. Her hair was frizzed from tape and dust. There was a line of little red marks across her wrist from bubble wrap.

"What?" she said, not mean, just tired.

I held up the gray coat. "Emergency."

"Is that Mrs. Leone's coat?"

"Yes. And in the pocket lining is the blue bird pin. Also, I am still mad at you. But this is bigger."

Tessa stared at me for one second, then grabbed her sneakers from beside the door.

We went together to 4B.

Mr. Leone answered after the third knock. He looked surprised to find the two of us standing there with a coat between us like an offering.

"I believe," I said, trying to sound official and not emotional, "that your bird was not lost. It was only hiding in a complicated manner."

Tessa reached into the pocket and carefully unclipped the pin. She set it in his palm.

For a moment he said nothing at all.

Then he laughed once, but it broke in the middle and became something else. He pressed the heel of his hand to his eye.

"Lucia," he said to the bird, not to us. "Of course. The coat pocket."

He looked up. "She used to put important things there because she said pockets were little houses."

None of us spoke.

Finally he pinned the bird back onto his cardigan, right over his heart, though it listed sideways because the knit was loose.

"There," he said.

Tessa asked, very quietly, "Were you donating the coat?"

He touched the sleeve. "Trying to. Failing to, perhaps. There is a difference."

Then he looked at us in the way old people sometimes do when they are seeing both children and something farther away at the same time.

"My girls," he said, "knowing where something is matters. Even if it is not where it used to be."

He thanked us three separate times, gave us each a peppermint, and told us to stop standing in the hall like abandoned furniture.

Back downstairs, Tessa and I sat behind the card table in the lobby. The service was open again, though business had slowed.

For a while we did not talk. Mrs. Gutierrez walked by and saluted us with a cucumber. The twins from 5C argued past the mailboxes. The building made all its usual noises, as if nothing in the world was changing, which was rude but normal.

Then Tessa said, "I was going to tell you Saturday night."

"That was a bad plan."

"I know."

"You should have told me when the first box showed up."

"I know."

I rubbed the edge of my sign with my thumb until some cereal cardboard fuzz came off. "How far?"

"Forty minutes."

"That is extremely far if you are nine."

"True."

We sat there another minute.

Then Tessa pulled a marker out of her pocket, flipped my sign over, and wrote her new address on the back in block letters.

"What are you doing?" I asked.

"In case you need to return me," she said.

I looked at the address. Apartment number, street, town. Real. Painfully real.

"I might," I said.

"I know."

So I wrote underneath it:

**NOT LOST. NEW LOCATION.**

Tessa snorted, and because she was still my best friend even in the middle of becoming harder to reach, I laughed too.

By the time my mother came down to call me up for dinner, the lobby light had turned gold and slanty, and the blue mitten was still unsolved, and Mr. Leone's bird was back where it belonged, even if slightly crooked.

That night I took the vice-president mitten and the investigation notebook up to my room. Before bed, I added one more entry.

**TESSA, 4D**  
**Not missing. Just going somewhere else.**  
**Address known.**

Then I slid the notebook into my desk drawer, which is a kind of pocket, and pockets, as it turns out, are little houses.
